Problem Description

What is Newsvendor Problem?

The newsvendor problem is to make a single-period procurement decision of a single product under stochastic demand

Newsvendor Framework:

  • One chance to decide on the stocking quantity of the product

  • Demand for the product is uncertain

  • Known marginal profit for each unit sold and known marginal loss for the ones that are bought and not sold

  • Goal : Maximize expected profit

 


Examples where the Newsvendor Framework is appropriate:

  • Perishable Goods
    • Meals in a Cafeteria

    • Dairy foods
  • Short selling season
    • Christmas trees, toys

    • Flowers on Valentine's day

    • Fashion clothes, seasonal clothes

    • Newspaper
 

 

Problem Description

 

  • Many supply chains in real world are decentralized in nature.
  • All members of such supply chains act independently and have conflicting objectives (Cachon, 2003)
  • If all the members of the supply chain act coherently, they can maximize their profits and the profit of the whole supply chain. But, it is difficult to put this ideal action practice (Wang, 2002)
  • Therefore, it is required to design incentive compatible mechanisms by which the objectives of different members of the supply chain are aligned and the system performance is improved

 

Decentralized vs Centralized Supply Chain

 

  • In a decentralised supply chain, every member of supply chain worries about maximising his/her own profits. Therefore, each member works as a stand alone business unit using a location optimisation strategy
  • In a centralised supply chain, every member tries to maximize his/her as well as of other members’ profit also. In this method, overall profit of supply chain gets maximised.

 

Theory of different contracts in decentralized supply chain

 

  • Supply Chain contracts are coordination mechanisms through which decentralised supply chains behave as closely as an integrated one
  • Types of Contracts
  • Price Only Contract: Do not coordinate but serve as a benchmark contract
  • Revenue Sharing Contract: The retailer pays a lower wholesale price to the manufacturer and shares a portion of the revenue
  • Buyback Contract: The retailer pays a higher wholesale price to the manufacturer but is assured some financial restitution from the manufacturer in the form of per unit buyback price.

 

Non coordinated decentralised setting

 

In a decentralised system, the manufacturer first announces his pricing policy and in response, the retailer makes his optimal pricing and order quantity decisions simultaneously.

Comparing Revenue sharing and Buy back Contracts:

 

Prs(Retailer) = p*S(q)-Co[Q-Z]*-Cu[Z-Q]*-Wrs*Q-(1-r)[p*min(Q,Z)]

Pbb(Retailer) = p*S(q)-Co[Q-Z]*-Cu[Z-Q]*-(Wbb)*Q+Pbb*[Q-Z]*

Consider a coordinating buy back contract. With that contract the retailer pays Wb-b for each unit purchased and an additional b per unit sold. (The common description for a buy back contract has the retailer paying Wb per unit purchased and receiving a credit of b per unit not sold, which is the same as paying Wb-b for each unit purchased no matter the demand realization and an additional b per unit sold.) With revenue sharing the retailer pays Wr+(f)sv. for each unit purchased and (f)(p-sv) for each unit sold.

So, we can map the corresponding parameters of a revenue sharing contract in a buyback contract to achieve approximately the same profit.
